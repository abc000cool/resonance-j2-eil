\title{Estimation-in-the-loop J2 formation-keeping: a Pareto
characterization of relative-navigation accuracy versus delta-V cost}
\author{The Resonance Foundation\thanks{administrator@theresonancefoundation.org}}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Spacecraft formation-flying missions in low Earth orbit must actively
suppress the differential drift induced by the $J_2$ zonal harmonic, and the
delta-V cost of doing so depends jointly on the controller design, the
fidelity of the relative-motion model, and, critically, on the accuracy of
the on-board relative navigation filter. Prior work --- most notably Koenig
\& D'Amico~\cite{koenig2018tradeoffs} --- has characterized this trade in a
linear time-invariant, near-circular, LQR-only setting with the estimator
represented as a static covariance floor. We extend that analysis to a
controller-and-plant-conditional Pareto surface using modern
quasi-nonsingular relative orbital element state transition matrices. We
implement three controllers (steady-state LQR on the Schweighart--Sedwick
plant; receding-horizon MPC on the Koenig--Guffanti--D'Amico ROE STM;
closed-form impulsive Chernick--D'Amico) and three relative-navigation
architectures (carrier-phase differential GPS, inter-satellite RF range,
angles-only optical) coupled through a Joseph-form error-state extended
Kalman filter, and drive the closed loop against a numerical $J_2$--$J_4$
truth propagator cross-validated by Orekit. Across a Monte-Carlo campaign
spanning inclination, formation size, and nav-accuracy sweeps, we
characterize the delta-V-versus-navigation-accuracy Pareto surface for each
controller, identify the certainty-equivalence-breakdown regime, and
quantify the fidelity trade between spending margin on nav accuracy versus
on plant model. The paper is accompanied by a fully reproducible open-source
implementation (MIT license).
\end{abstract}
